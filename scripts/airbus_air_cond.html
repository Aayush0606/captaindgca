#80. Once set to “ON” the air conditioning packs operate:

The two packs operate automatically and independently of each other. Pack operation is controlled by signals coming from the pack controller or the Air Conditioning System Controller (ACSC). Warm pre-conditioned bleed air enters the cooling path via the pack flow control valve, and is ducted to the primary heat exchanger. Then, the cooled bleed air enters the compressor section of the air-cycle machine and is compressed to a higher pressure and temperature. It is cooled again in the main heat exchanger and enters the turbine section, where it expands and, in expanding, generates power to drive the compressor and cooling air fan. The removal of energy during this process reduces air temperature, resulting in very low air temperature at turbine discharge. L3 A water separator system dries the air before it enters the turbine section.

#106. After the engine start, the Pack Flow Control Valves automatically open, however on the ground, reopening of the valves is delayed for _____ after the first engine start.

After the engine start, the Pack Flow Control Valves automatically open. However, on the ground, the reopening of these valves is delayed for a specific period after the first engine start, depending on the aircraft’s Manufacturer Serial Number (MSN) range.

Specifically:

  • For aircraft within the MSN ranges of 02624-02907, 03271-03305, 03344, 03551, 03620-03687, 03822-03918, 03970, 04020-04052, 04089, 04121, 04164-04237, and 06446-06803, the reopening of the valves on the ground is delayed for 30 seconds to prevent an additional pack closure cycle during the second engine start.
  • For aircraft within the MSN ranges of 07459-08167 and 08202-08917, this delay is extended to 60 seconds for the same reason.

The purpose of this delay is to avoid an unnecessary pack closure cycle when the second engine is started. The pack flow control valve typically closes immediately on the starting engine side if the crossbleed valve is closed, when the engine mode selector is set to IGN (or CRK), and remains closed until N2 is less than 50%. If the crossbleed valve is open during engine start, both pack flow control valves will close.

#107. The Ram Air Inlet Flaps remain open during takeoff and landing to allow extra airflow during high power situations.

According to the sources, the ram air inlet flap’s behaviour during takeoff and landing is as follows:

  • The ram air inlet flap closes during takeoff and landing.
  • The primary reason for this closure is to avoid the ingestion of foreign matter.
  • During takeoff, the flap closes when takeoff power is set and the main landing gear struts are compressed.

Therefore, the flaps are designed to close, not remain open, during these high-power phases to protect the system.

#115. There are three Trim Air valves, one for Cockpit zone and one each for FWD and AFT cabin zones. Trim air valves optimize the temperature by:

The trim air valves play a crucial role in optimising the temperature within each aircraft zone. As you’ve noted, there is one trim air valve associated with each zone: the Cockpit, Forward Cabin, and Aft Cabin.

Here’s how they optimise the temperature:

 

  • The Air Conditioning System Controllers (ACSC) or the zone controller control these valves electrically.
  • Their primary function is to adjust the temperature by adding hot air. This hot air is tapped from upstream of the packs.
  • Specifically, the cockpit trim air valve is controlled by ACSC 1, while the cabin trim air valves (for FWD and AFT cabin) are controlled by ACSC 2.

In essence, the trim air valves fine-tune the temperature for individual zones by mixing precise amounts of hot air with the conditioned air supplied from the mixing unit. This allows the system to achieve and maintain the selected temperature for each of the independently controlled zones.

#156. If both blower and extract p/b switches are set to OVRD, during smoke drill, the avionic equipment air is ……

When both the BLOWER and the EXTRACT pushbuttons are set to the OVRD position during a smoke drill, the following occurs for the avionics equipment air:

  • The air conditioning system supplies cooling air, which is then exhausted overboard.
  • The avionics ventilation system transitions into a closed circuit configuration.
  • The blower fan stops.
  • The extract fan continues to run.

#157. What happens during smoke drill when both the blower and the extract p/b switches are set to override.

When you press the Extract P/B to OVRD (Override) on the overhead panel, regardless of external conditions like heavy rain on the ground, the following actions occur:

  • Extract Fan keeps running. Source explicitly states: “When the EXTRACT pushbutton switch is set at OVRD, the extract fan is controlled directly from the pushbutton. Both fans continue to run.”
  • Air-conditioning air is added for ventilation. When either the BLOWER or EXTRACT pushbutton switch is in the OVRD position, “Air from the air conditioning system is added to ventilation air”.
  • The Avionics ventilation system goes into Closed Config. When either the BLOWER or EXTRACT pushbutton switch is in the OVRD position, “The system goes to closed circuit configuration”.

Therefore, options 2, 3, and 4 describe what happens. Option 1, “Extract Fan stops,” is incorrect. The condition of “heavy rain on ground” does not change these override behaviors, as the OVRD selection forces the system into a specific configuration, bypassing automatic controls that might otherwise be influenced by external conditions.

#163. You have set the Thrust Levers to FLX/MCT for take-off and OAT is +40°C. What is the configuration of avionics ventilation?

When the Thrust Levers are set to FLX/MCT for takeoff and the Outside Air Temperature (OAT) is +40°C, the avionics ventilation system will be in the closed-circuit configuration.

Here’s why:

  • The avionics ventilation system operates automatically.
  • When the BLOWER and EXTRACT pushbutton switches are in the normal “AUTO” position, the system’s configuration depends on the operational phase.
  • During ground operations after the application of takeoff power, the ventilation system automatically transitions to a closed-circuit configuration. This condition applies when the thrust levers are set to FLX/MCT for takeoff.
  • While an OAT of +40°C is high and might typically lead to an open-circuit configuration during general ground operations (where the skin temperature is above +12°C), the specific action of applying takeoff power (setting thrust levers to FLX/MCT) overrides other ground-based temperature thresholds, forcing the system into the closed-circuit configuration for the takeoff phase.

#165. What is the meaning of CAB PR LDG ELEV FAULT?

The “CAB PR LDG ELEV FAULT” message is an ECAM alert that indicates a fault in the aircraft’s systems related to cabin pressure and landing elevation management.

This message specifically appears in situations involving failures of the Flight Management and Guidance Computers (FMGCs):

  • It may be triggered by the Flight Warning System (FWS) in the event of a single latch FMGC failure. A “single latch” occurs when one FMGC fails and latches, leading to single mode operation.
  • It is displayed when both FMGCs become inoperative (dual latch), indicating a loss of FM and FG capability, along with other critical system failures.
  • The message is also presented after a dual FMGS reset with loss of data and autorecovery, particularly after four successive dual resets within 3 minutes, which results in the erasure of all pilot-entered data.

#166. With Pack 1 and 2 Fault, the recommended max FL is

Based on the provided sources, there is no explicit mention of a recommended maximum flight level (FL) specifically for a “Pack 1 and 2 Fault”.

However, the sources do provide information about:

  • Recommended Maximum Altitude (REC MAX): This altitude is displayed on the PROG page and is the lowest maximum altitude the aircraft can reach while maintaining a 0.3 g buffet margin, flying level at MAX CRZ rating, or maintaining a V/S of 300 ft/min at MAX CLB thrust, among other criteria. It is limited to FL 398. Anti-ice is not considered for this computation.
  • Engine-Out Condition: If one engine is out, the REC MAX altitude displayed on the PROG page is the recommended maximum engine-out altitude, which is computed based on the long-range cruise speed and assumes anti-ice is off.

While the FCOM excerpts detail how engine-out conditions affect the recommended maximum altitude, and they mention pack fault indications, they do not specify a corresponding maximum flight level for a complete loss of both air conditioning packs.
